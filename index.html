02 / Origin

Ayvalık. 38.7° north.

"Three thousand years of cultivation. One harvest. No compromises."

Ayvalık sits at the northern edge of the olive's natural growing range. Cool nights slow the ripening of the Ayvalık variety — concentrating polyphenols, building a complexity that warm-climate oils rarely achieve. It is not the easiest place to grow olives. That is precisely the point.

We harvest at 10–20% ripeness — before the olive has completed its sugar cycle — to fix free acidity below 0.3% and capture a bitterness and pepper finish that professional palates recognise immediately. The oil is pressed cold, within four hours of leaving the grove. No overnight storage. No fermentation. No shortcuts.

The result is not wellness oil. It is not a lifestyle product. It is the ingredient a chef reaches for when the dish depends on it.

Origin & Science · October 2025

The case for early harvest — why we pick before peak ripeness

Most olive oil producers wait. They let the Ayvalık fruit ripen fully because a mature olive gives more volume per tree. The economic logic is obvious. The quality logic runs the other way.

At 10–20% ripeness, before the olive has completed its sugar and fatty-acid cycle, two things are true simultaneously: free acidity is at its lowest, and polyphenol concentration is at its highest. These two values move in opposite directions as the fruit matures.

The chemistry of the green fruit

Free acidity in olive oil is a measure of degradation. A freshly picked, undamaged green olive has minimal enzymatic activity. Its free fatty acid content is correspondingly low. By the time the same fruit reaches full ripeness, enzymatic activity has been running for weeks.

Polyphenols — specifically oleocanthal, oleuropein, and hydroxytyrosol — are the compounds responsible for the peppery, bitter finish that distinguishes premium EVOO. Early-picked Ayvalık Olive Oil registers polyphenol concentrations above 300 mg/kg. The same grove's oil at full ripeness: typically 80–120 mg/kg.

The yield trade-off

We lose volume. An early-picked tree gives roughly 12–15% oil by weight. A fully ripe fruit gives 18–22%. Over a grove of several thousand trees, the difference is significant. We accept it because the alternative is an oil that meets the letter of the IOC's extra virgin standard while failing its spirit.

"The early pick is a decision to make less oil and better oil."

KALA's October harvest is the only harvest we run. One press window. One specification.

Quality & Specification · November 2025

What 0.2% free acidity actually means on the pass

The number 0.2% appears on the KALA tin, on every batch certificate, and in every conversation we have with buyers. The International Olive Council sets 0.8% as the maximum free acidity permitted for extra virgin classification. It is a generous ceiling.

What free acidity is, precisely

Free acidity is not a flavour compound directly — it is a marker of degradation. Oleic acid remains bound in triglyceride form in an intact, freshly pressed olive. The moment degradation begins, fatty acids cleave from their glycerol backbone and become "free."

An oil at 0.7% has undergone significantly more degradation than an oil at 0.2%. The palate detects this indirectly: as a flat, slightly greasy quality in the mid-palate, a short finish, an absence of structural bitterness.

"The difference between 0.4% and 0.2% is a quality decision made at every point between the grove and the tin."

Every KALA batch is independently tested and certified. The number does not shift between seasons because the decisions that produce it — early pick, same-day press, cold extraction — do not shift either.

Geography · December 2025

Ayvalık: why 38.7° north latitude is the point

Ayvalık is not the intuitive choice. The olive's heartland runs from Andalusia through Tunisia to the Levant. Ayvalık sits at the northern edge of the olive's viable range. The winters are cooler, the growing season is shorter.

Stress physiology and flavour

Plants under moderate environmental stress produce secondary metabolites as a protective response. In olives, that response manifests primarily as polyphenol synthesis. An Ayvalık olive grown in cooler northern conditions builds polyphenols at a higher rate than the same variety grown in a warmer climate.

"The grove has been here for three thousand years. We are simply the latest people to understand what it produces when you harvest it correctly."

The geography is not incidental to the specification. It is the specification.

Kitchen Application · January 2026

Cooking with high-polyphenol oil — what the heat does

The standard advice is to keep premium extra virgin olive oil away from heat. This advice was written for standard commercial oil. For high-polyphenol oil, it is partially wrong.

Why polyphenols matter at temperature

Polyphenols in EVOO function as natural antioxidants. Their role in the pan is analogous to their role in the bottle: they scavenge free radicals produced during heating, protecting the oil's fatty-acid structure from oxidation. An oil with a high polyphenol load degrades more slowly under heat.

"The finishing argument has been overstated. A structurally sound oil handles a hot pan differently to a fragile one."

We do not claim that KALA performs as a neutral cooking oil. We claim its specification makes it more versatile than the standard advice suggests.

Supply Chain · February 2026

Why four hours matters — the oxidation window

The moment an olive breaks from its branch, degradation begins. Cell walls rupture under handling. Polyphenol oxidase begins converting polyphenols into melanin. Lipase enzymes begin cleaving fatty acids, incrementally raising free acidity.

Free acidity increases by roughly 0.03–0.06 percentage points per hour under ambient Aegean October conditions. Polyphenol concentration falls an estimated 8–12% per four-hour window beyond the first press opportunity.

"Every hour between the grove and the press is a decision — to prioritise logistics over specification."

The four-hour rule requires running the press in shifts during harvest. We chose the harder schedule.
